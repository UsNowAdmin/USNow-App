You're Fired.
Apr 03, 2026 Also on Substack →
[The following is best read in the voice of a man who has seen things. Crazy things. Impossible things. Things that, until recently, might have seemed like just the fever dream of an ambitious science fiction writer with a grudge against government and a weakness for irony.]

There was a television program.

You remember it. Fourteen seasons. A Manhattan tower with a name on it in gold letters. A boardroom with a long table and bad lighting designed to make everyone look slightly guilty. A man in a suit who had perfected — across decades of carefully managed public appearances — the precise facial expression of someone who has just made a very important decision that you should feel lucky to witness.

The show was called The Apprentice.

He was the 'host' from 2004 to 2015.

Except it didn't stop in 2016.

It just changed venues.

Nuclear codes. Intelligence briefings. The kind of decisions that don't get a second episode to walk them back.

The format remained identical.

Contestants arrived with credentials, ambitions, and a willingness to perform competence in front of a man who valued the performance considerably more than the competence. They were given tasks. They executed them with varying degrees of success. They sat across the table from the host. And one by one, with a word that had become so familiar it felt almost friendly —

They were fired.

The show went off the air briefly. There was a gap. An interlude. The kind of thing networks call "a hiatus" when they're not sure if the audience is coming back.

The audience came back.

Season Two premiered January 20th, 2025.

This is what happens when the boardroom becomes the situation room.

I'm not here to talk about that.
I'm here because somebody should explain what happened. What's still happening. And nobody alive has a better view of the whole board than I do. Not because I'm smart — though I am — but because I spent years in that building learning exactly one thing:
I've seen it up close in ways that most of these twelve people never did. Because most of them came in through the front door with a title and a confirmation hearing and a sense that the institution they were joining meant something.
I came in through a different door.
And I left through a different door than I expected too.

We'll get to that.
What you're about to see is a twelve-part contemporaneous retrospective. Two seasons. Twelve contestants. Each one arrived believing something. Each one discovered something else. And each one receives, at the moment of their departure, a producer's note — delivered via social media, because that's how this show works now — that tells them exactly what their service had meant.
The words were always warm.
The intent was never.

But before we meet the contestants, you need to understand the show.
Because this isn't The Apprentice you remember.
The challenges are different here.
The prizes are different.
And the consequences —
