Flynn Nat'l Security Advisor ◆ FIRED · Season 1 ▼ UNSEAL FILE

Lied to the FBI about Russia. Then begged for a pardon. Then led "Lock Her Up" chants at the RNC anyway.

Michael Flynn lasted 24 days as National Security Advisor — the shortest tenure in history. He lied to FBI agents about his conversations with Russian Ambassador Sergey Kislyak, pled guilty, flipped on Trump, then un-flipped, then begged for a pardon. He got it. He then went on to suggest the U.S. should have a single national religion and that Myanmar's military coup should be replicated in America. He said this at a QAnon conference. He brought his own microphone.

FIRED: Day 24 · Pardoned: Dec. 2020 Source: DOJ · FBI · 2017–2020

Bannon Chief Strategist ◆ FIRED · Season 1 ▼ UNSEAL FILE

Indicted for fraud. Went to prison. Got out. Immediately started another podcast.

Steve Bannon, the architect of MAGA's political identity, was fired after giving an interview calling himself "the real president." He was then indicted for defrauding donors to a private "We Build the Wall" fundraiser — pocketing money meant for border security. He was convicted, sentenced to 4 months in federal prison, served it, and returned to his War Room podcast before the ink on his release papers was dry. He called prison "a resort." The other inmates were reportedly not consulted.

FIRED: Aug. 2017 · Imprisoned: 2024 Source: SDNY · FBI · War Room Podcast
405 Days
Tillerson Sec. of State ◆ FIRED · Season 1 ▼ UNSEAL FILE

Called Trump a "moron." Got fired by tweet. From a bathroom. In Kenya.

Rex Tillerson, former ExxonMobil CEO and one-time Secretary of State, reportedly told colleagues that Trump was a "moron" after a Pentagon meeting. He never fully denied it. In March 2018, while on a diplomatic trip to Africa, he woke up to learn via Twitter that he had been fired — reportedly while in or near a bathroom at his hotel in Kenya. Trump announced his replacement had already been chosen. Tillerson later said Trump "acts on instinct" without being "fully informed." He was being diplomatic.

FIRED: March 2018 · Via Tweet · From Kenya Source: NBC News · State Dept. · 2018
708 Days
Sessions Attorney General ◆ FIRED · Season 1 ▼ UNSEAL FILE

Was Trump's most loyal ally. Recused himself. Spent two years being publicly humiliated on Twitter.

Jeff Sessions was the first senator to endorse Trump in 2016 — a risk that electrified the campaign. As Attorney General, he recused himself from the Russia investigation as legally required. Trump never forgave him. For nearly two years, Trump publicly attacked Sessions on Twitter — calling him "weak," "beleaguered," and "disgraceful." Sessions endured it all in silence. The day after the 2018 midterms, Trump fired him by letter. Sessions later ran for his old Senate seat. Trump destroyed him in the primary. He lost to Tommy Tuberville.

FIRED: Nov. 7, 2018 · By letter · Day after midterms Source: DOJ · Twitter archive · 2017–2018
571 Days
Kelly Chief of Staff ◆ FIRED · Season 1 ▼ UNSEAL FILE

Four-star general. Said Trump is "the most flawed human being I have ever met." Voted for him anyway.

John Kelly, retired Marine general and Gold Star father, became Chief of Staff to bring discipline to chaos. He later described the White House as near-constant disorder and said Trump surrounded himself with "yes-men." In 2024, Kelly publicly called Trump "a fascist to the core" and said he was "the most flawed person" he'd ever encountered. Trump called Kelly "the dumbest of my military guys." Then, in November 2024, John Kelly voted for Donald Trump anyway. The general had his reasons. He kept them to himself.

RESIGNED: Dec. 2018 · Voted for him: Nov. 2024 Source: Atlantic · NYT · 2024 election
688 Days
Mattis Sec. of Defense ◆ RESIGNED · Season 1 ▼ UNSEAL FILE

Resigned with a letter so brutal it was assigned reading in political science classes.

General James "Mad Dog" Mattis — the Warrior Monk, the most decorated Marine of his generation — resigned as Secretary of Defense in December 2018 after Trump announced a sudden withdrawal from Syria without consulting him. His resignation letter was a methodical, devastating rebuke: allies must be treated with respect, adversaries must be held accountable, and the President deserved a Secretary of Defense whose views were "more aligned." Trump said Mattis was "essentially fired." Mattis did not respond. He went home and read Stoic philosophy. This is documented.

RESIGNED: Dec. 20, 2018 · Letter still circulates Source: Pentagon · DOD · Resignation letter, 2018

Texted war plans to a Signal chat. His wife was in it. So was his personal lawyer.

Bondi Attorney General ◇ SEASON 2 · ACTIVE ▼ UNSEAL FILE

A mass shooting survivor asked about gun reform. She answered with the stock market.

At a Senate hearing, Attorney General Bondi was questioned by a Parkland survivor's parent about gun violence policy. Her response wandered — inexplicably — to the performance of the stock market under Trump. The room went silent. The internet did not. The moment became shorthand for a certain genre of Washington non-answer. She was confirmed 54–45 and is now the nation's top law enforcement officer.

STATUS: Confirmed 54–45 · Market up Source: Senate Judiciary Hearing · Feb. 2025

Gabbard Dir. of Nat'l Intel. ◇ SEASON 2 · ACTIVE ▼ UNSEAL FILE

Ran for President as a progressive. Now runs the spy agencies she once called corrupt.

Tulsi Gabbard spent her congressional career denouncing the surveillance state, calling U.S. intelligence agencies liars, and defending Bashar al-Assad's Syria on Fox News. She visited Assad personally. She introduced legislation to stop arming "terrorists." She ran for president as a Democrat in 2020. By 2024 she had switched parties entirely. She now serves as Director of National Intelligence — overseeing the very agencies she spent a decade calling corrupt, dishonest, and dangerous. The receipts are on C-SPAN. All of them. In HD.

STATUS: Confirmed · In charge of the deep state Source: Congressional record · DNI confirmation Jan. 2025

RFK Jr. Sec. of HHS ◇ SEASON 2 · ACTIVE ▼ UNSEAL FILE

Spent 30 years warning about vaccines. Now runs public health. Measles came back on his watch.

Robert F. Kennedy Jr. built his entire public identity around vaccine skepticism, pharmaceutical distrust, and institutional health agency corruption. He sued the CDC. He wrote books warning about government health capture. He ran for president on those beliefs. He is now Secretary of Health and Human Services — head of the agencies he spent decades calling criminal. In the spring of 2025, measles outbreaks spread across multiple U.S. states for the first time in decades. He also disclosed in a legal filing that a worm had eaten part of his brain and died there. He described this as "not a concern." Doctors disagreed.

STATUS: In office · Measles: also in office Source: HHS · CDC · Court filing, 2012 · 2025 outbreak data
