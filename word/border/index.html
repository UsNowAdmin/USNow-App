Border

A line on a map, a security concept, or a human rights crisis.
How We Say It
bor·dər
Where It Comes From
Old French bordure — edge of a shield. From border, to border. Originally referred to the decorative edge of a textile or heraldic device before it became a political term for territorial limits.
How It's Been Used
Militarized and politicized most intensely in the 20th century as nation-states hardened. Before that, borders were porous, contested, and often irrelevant to the people living near them. Today the word carries the full weight of immigration policy, nationalism, and human movement.
