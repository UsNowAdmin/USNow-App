Debate

Historically, a method of sharing and testing opposite or incompatible points of view — used to obtain more knowledge, consensus, and/or resolution. Now, often just theater.
How We Say It
di·bayt
Where It Comes From
Old French debatre — to fight, contend. From Latin battuere — to beat, strike. Related to battle and combat. The word carries its martial origin — debate as verbal combat. Formal debate as structured exchange traces to medieval university disputation and ancient Greek and Roman rhetoric.
How It's Been Used
Codified across institutions — parliamentary debate, presidential debates, competitive debate in schools, Oxford-style formats. The expectation that debate produces clarity through opposed argument has eroded as televised political debates have shifted toward performance, soundbites, and avoidance. Scholarly study of deliberation distinguishes genuine debate from positional posturing.
