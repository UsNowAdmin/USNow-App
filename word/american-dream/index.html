American Dream

The promise that effort equals outcome — more myth than mechanism.
How We Say It
ə·mer·i·kən dreem
Where It Comes From
Coined by historian James Truslow Adams in 1931 in The Epic of America — during the Depression. Originally meant a social order where each person could achieve the fullest stature of which they are capable, regardless of birth.
How It's Been Used
Quickly narrowed from Adams' broad civic vision to a economic promise: work hard, get ahead, own a home. Used by politicians across the spectrum as shorthand for upward mobility. Increasingly invoked as critique — a myth that justifies inequality by making failure personal.
