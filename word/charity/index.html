Charity

Generosity, tax strategy, or substitute for justice.
How We Say It
char·i·tee
Where It Comes From
Old French charité, from Latin caritas — love, esteem. In early Christian usage it referred to the highest form of love — love of God and neighbor — before narrowing to mean giving money to the poor.
How It's Been Used
Descended from theological virtue to tax category. The gap between its origins (love) and its current function (giving away surplus) is a measure of what the word lost. Critics argue charity substitutes for structural justice; defenders argue it fills gaps the state won't.
