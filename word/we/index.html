We

The most dangerous two-letter word. Born from ego and the need for security. To them, we is they. (see also They)
How We Say It
wee
Where It Comes From
Old English wē — first-person plural pronoun. From Proto-Germanic *wīz. Related to Sanskrit vayam, suggesting Indo-European antiquity. The political use of 'we' — to assert collective identity or speak for others — is universal and central to rhetoric of nationalism, religion, and movement-building.
How It's Been Used
The 'royal we' used by monarchs and popes elevates speech with collective authority. The 'editorial we' performs impersonality. 'We the people' — the opening of the U.S. Constitution — claims democratic authority. Political analysis of 'we' often turns on who is included and who is excluded by the pronoun's apparent inclusiveness.
