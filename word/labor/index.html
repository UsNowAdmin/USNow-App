Labor

The individual or collective workforce that produces goods and services — including better technology that reduces the need for labor. (see also Work)
How We Say It
lay·bər
Where It Comes From
Latin labor — toil, exertion, hardship, suffering. The double meaning — work and the pain of work — is ancient. Entered English through Old French labour. The specifically obstetric sense — labor as childbirth — preserves the older meaning of painful exertion.
How It's Been Used
Central to political economy from Adam Smith through Marx — the labor theory of value held that human labor is the source of economic value. 'Labor' as a collective political actor — organized labor, the labor movement — distinguishes itself from 'work' which can be individual. The Labor Party in Britain and labor parties in other democracies institutionalize the worker as a political subject.
