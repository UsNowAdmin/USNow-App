Reconstruction

The period after the Civil War when the US attempted constitutional enforcement and multiracial democracy — and failed at least both.
How We Say It
ree·kun·STRUK·shun
Where It Comes From
Used to describe the rebuilding of the Confederate states after the Civil War. The formal Reconstruction era is dated 1865-1877 — from the war's end to the Compromise of 1877, which resolved the disputed presidential election by withdrawing federal troops from the South.
How It's Been Used
The most contested period in American historiography. For a generation, the dominant narrative — shaped by Southern historians and the Lost Cause tradition — portrayed Reconstruction as a period of Northern oppression and Black incompetence. Revisionist historians from the 1960s onward, led by W.E.B. Du Bois and later Eric Foner, documented it as a genuine democratic experiment — Black political participation, constitutional amendments, public education — betrayed by the withdrawal of federal enforcement. The contemporary understanding is closer to the revisionist account.
