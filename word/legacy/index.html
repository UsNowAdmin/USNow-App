Legacy

What we leave behind and what we're blamed for.
How We Say It
leg·ə·see
Where It Comes From
Medieval Latin legatia — office of a legate, then bequest. From legare — to send as a deputy, to bequeath. Originally strictly about property left at death. The broader sense — what one leaves behind in terms of influence or reputation — is a modern extension.
How It's Been Used
Politicians, athletes, and executives are evaluated constantly on their 'legacy' — an accounting that happens before and after death. The word implies that actions will be judged by a future that may have different values. 'Legacy admission' to universities repurposes the word to describe inherited privilege.
