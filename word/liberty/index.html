Liberty

Freedom to learn and freedom to act without harm. (see also Freedom)
How We Say It
lib·ər·tee
Where It Comes From
Latin libertas — freedom, the condition of a free person. From liber — free. In Roman law, a specific legal status — the opposite of slavery. The Statue of Liberty, the Liberty Bell — the word carries a formal, civic weight that 'freedom' does not always.
How It's Been Used
In American political philosophy, liberty tends toward the negative — freedom from interference, especially from government. The word has been claimed most insistently by libertarian and conservative movements. 'Civil liberties' is a different usage — specific protections against state power.
